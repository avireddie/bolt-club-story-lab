Page 1: The Bolt Club Arrives

The Bolt Club’s bus rolled into Chhatrapati Mini Rail Museum just as monsoon clouds turned the old platform silver.

Soham, Build Captain, carried a tin box with his Pocket Periscope Rover inside—a palm-size car with a folding mirror neck.

Kabir read every card twice; Rohan chased footprints; Tara spotted tiny words that changed cases; Meera guarded jalebi-popcorn, clips, chalk, a mini torch, and string.

Today’s toy-engine parade needed the brass Signal-Tower Lantern on the front carriage—but someone had already shouted that it was gone.

Volunteer Rafiq uncle stood by the ticket-shed counter while three Platform Panthers pointed at him.

The second horn would blow in twelve minutes, and if the lantern stayed missing, the parade would be canceled.

Page 2: The Horn That Stopped

Earlier that morning, volunteers had run a snack-tent cheer contest; the brass café bell was part of the decoration—everything tagged with return cards for later.

A whistle squealed, and the parade manager shouted, “Stop the engine! The Signal-Tower Lantern is gone.”

One Panther captain, Nikhil, said, “We saw brass in his hand before the first horn.” He shook a folded scrap—his fast copied helper checklist from setup.

Kabir read the official setup card on the empty hook:

Before first horn: put all loose brass props in Parcel Chest 3, except the Signal-Tower Lantern on the hook.

Tara whispered, “That sign has a tiny word with superhero muscles.”

Meera tightened her pouch while everyone stared at the brass bell in Rafiq uncle’s hand.

Page 3: The Wrong Brass Thing

Rohan ran to the café stall and came back breathing like a steam engine.

“The snack tent used a brass bell for the cheer contest,” he said, “and its card says Rafiq uncle had to carry it to the ticket-shed counter.”

The card at the café stall read:

Café bell and bunting: return to ticket-shed counter before first horn.

Nikhil crossed his arms. “Brass is brass. He took the lantern.”

Kabir shook his head. “No. The card says café bell, not lantern.”

Soham crouched near the ticket-shed grill; behind it stood Parcel Chest 3, a wooden chest with railway stickers and a lid that did not fully shut.

A fat roll of old tickets was trapped under one corner of the lid, leaving a thin dark slit.

A floor gap ran under the locked grill, but it was too low for a hand and just high enough for a small toy car.

Meera finally opened her jalebi-popcorn, took exactly three pieces for courage, and gave Soham a binder clip and her mini torch.

Tara told the Panthers, “Please keep pointing dramatically; it helps us measure how wrong you are.”

Page 4: The Rover Under the Grill

Soham unfolded the Pocket Periscope Rover’s mirror neck and clipped Meera’s torch to its roof.

The rover was flatter than Meera’s snack box, so it slipped under the grill without touching the bars.

Soham pushed the joystick forward; the rover rolled across the dusty floor toward Parcel Chest 3.

The mirror neck bumped the chest once, folded back, and Soham stopped before it cracked.

“Upgrade,” Meera said, and clipped a short piece of string to the mirror neck so Soham could pull it upright gently after the rover stopped.

Soham drove again, stopped beside the ticket-roll corner, and pulled the string; the mirror rose into the thin slit under the lid.

The torch beam bounced from mirror to brass, and a label flashed in the reflection:

SIGNAL-TOWER LANTERN — KEEP ON HOOK.

Kabir grinned. “That proves the lantern is inside Parcel Chest 3.”

Rohan sprinted to the parade manager before the cancellation bell could ring.

Tara raised both hands. “Permission to be extremely smug?”

Page 5: The Tiny Word That Saved the Parade

The parade manager unlocked the grill, lifted the chest lid, and pulled out the brass lantern by its cool black handle.

Nikhil’s face dropped like a biscuit into chai.

In his fist was the same crumpled helper checklist from setup; it read:

All loose brass props → Parcel Chest 3 before first horn.

Kabir placed it beside the official setup card and read both slowly.

“Official card: all loose brass props go in the chest, except the Signal-Tower Lantern. Nikhil’s copy skipped except.”

Nikhil muttered, “I thought I was helping set up faster.”

Rafiq uncle lifted the café bell. “This is what I carried before the first horn.”

Soham clipped the lantern to the toy engine’s front hook, and the hook clicked shut around the handle.

The second horn blew, the toy engine rolled, and the lantern threw a golden stripe across the wet platform.

Meera opened the train-sticker packet for everyone, while Tara announced, “Today’s lesson: never fight a tiny word. It carries a wrench.”
